Legal Foundation — In Short

Exaction:

a condition the government attaches to a permit — dedicate land, pay an impact fee, fund off-site improvements. The Takings Clause of the Fifth Amendment as applied to the states through the Fourteenth creates the question of when a condition becomes an unconstitutional taking.

The case sequence.

Nollan v. California Coastal Commission (1987) required an essential nexus between the exaction and the government's stated regulatory interest.

Dolan v. City of Tigard (1994) added rough proportionality in nature and extent.

Koontz v. St. Johns River Water Management District (2013) extended Nollan/Dolan to denied permits and to monetary demands.

Sheetz v. County of El Dorado (2024) held that California courts had been reviewing development impact fee schedules too leniently to satisfy the constitutional standard.

The doctrine only protects the landowner who says no.

Enforcement requires someone willing to walk away from their permit and litigate. In practice, the constitutional test only activates when someone fights — and people almost never fight, because a lawsuit costs $500,000 or more, takes years, and risks the entire project.

Tax treatment layers on top of doctrine.

Under IRS Revenue Ruling 2002-9 (building on Oriole Homes Corp. v. U.S.), impact fees required for subdivision approval are capitalized as a development cost and deducted pro rata as homes sell. The IRS reasoned that the fees increase the value of the subdivision and secure a benefit lasting beyond the taxable year. Treatment varies by industry: housing developers defer deductions to sale; telecom expenses franchise fees immediately; oil, gas, and renewables deduct against operations; cannabis, uniquely, is blocked from most deductions under Section 280E.

LIHTC and Section 8 — a double-extraction mechanism.

The Low-Income Housing Tax Credit allocates federal tax credits to developers who build affordable units; developers sell those credits to investors, typically financial institutions, who take a 99% ownership stake. When Section 8 rental assistance attaches to LIHTC units, the federal taxpayer funds the building twice: first as foregone tax revenue to finance construction, then as monthly rent subsidy paid to the operator for the life of the property. The tenant is not the customer. The government is the customer. The tenant is the commodity the government pays the operator to house. (Working hypothesis.)

Identified Patterns

My current working observations.

1. Exactions and public utilities are not the exact same extraction mechanism, but they run on structurally similar ones. Both use legal instruments that shift liability off the operator, both pair private revenue with governmental funding, both rely on mandate or permit conditions to secure captive revenue, and both have essentially no recourse for failed, over-budget, or otherwise unsatisfactory service or product.

2. The mechanism is protected from unified scrutiny because its components live in separate legal regimes — federal takings doctrine, tax court, state property and HOA law. No single forum has jurisdiction over the whole pattern. The same separation exists at the legislative and executive levels, where different state and federal committees, agencies, and statutes govern different pieces of the mechanism. The fragmentation enables a compounding effect that obscures how the system functions.

3. Constitutional doctrine (Nollan / Dolan / Koontz / Sheetz) addresses a narrow slice of the actual extraction mechanics. Most of the mechanism operates outside the reach of the doctrine that ostensibly governs it. Legislative deference does not solve this, because the legislature is itself incentivized to favor exaction and utility companies — the costs are borne downstream by ratepayers, buyers, and the larger tax base, and the benefits are delivered up front as infrastructure and development.

4. The ultimate cost falls on end buyers, ratepayers, and the larger tax base who had no seat at the table when the decisions that created the system were made. This is compounded by geographic lock-in: utilities operate in monopoly service territories, and housing costs are tied to specific local markets. Most people bearing the cost have limited meaningful choice in location, which further shields the system from the normal market pressures of capitalism.

Jurisdictional Fragmentation

An open question.

The mechanisms above sit across three different legal regimes, each with its own court system and none with shared jurisdiction over the others:

  • Constitutional takings doctrine (Nollan / Dolan / Koontz / Sheetz) — federal courts, Article III.
  • Tax treatment (Rev. Rul. 2002-9, Section 280E, transfer pricing between affiliated entities) — Tax Court under Article I, federal district courts, and IRS administrative proceedings.
  • Property law, HOA governance, CC&Rs, declarant control, utility rate-setting — state courts, and in many cases state Article II administrative bodies.

A takings challenge addresses the exaction layer. A tax dispute addresses the deduction layer. An HOA governance action addresses the lock-in layer. Each forum has jurisdiction over its own slice and no authority over the others.

The observation I am working through is this: I am not sure the mechanism is protected despite this fragmentation. I think it may be protected by it. The whole pattern cannot be brought into any single courtroom, because no court has jurisdiction over the whole pattern. That would mean the structural shielding is an emergent feature of how American law allocates jurisdiction, not something anyone designed.

This is the part that is most convoluted and new to me. The incentive structures and the individual mechanisms I am familiar with. The jurisdictional split as a pattern is newer to me, and I am not confident I am reading it correctly.
