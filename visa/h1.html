Visa Machine · H-1

H-1

Universities

Major research universities are cap-exempt H-1B sponsors — they can hire without entering the annual lottery. They also serve as the primary pipeline converting international F-1 students into H-1B workers at corporate sponsors. They receive substantial funding from the same companies that benefit from this pipeline.

  • Educational Services: 15,941 H-1B initial approvals FY2025 — second highest industry
  • 25 major research universities each sponsor 100+ H-1B workers annually
  • Stanford: 329 initial approvals FY2025 · MIT: 104 · Harvard: 112 · Yale: 108
  • Stanford: 36% of graduate students are international
  • TCS: $35M gift to Carnegie Mellon 2015 — to strengthen industry-academia talent partnerships
  • Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft fund university AI/ML centers and endowed professorships
  • University lobbying 2025: record spending — Harvard spent nearly $1M
  • F-1 OPT graduates frequently transition directly to H-1B at sponsoring companies
Tech Direct Sponsors

Amazon, Meta, Microsoft, and Google are the top four H-1B direct sponsors in FY2025 — a structural shift from prior years when Indian outsourcing firms dominated. Unlike outsourcing models, these companies sponsor H-1B workers in genuine specialty roles, predominantly at Master's degree level. The visa tether still applies: job loss triggers the 60-day deportation clock.

  • Amazon: 4,644 initial FY2025 (+20% YoY) · 14,532 continuing · ~19,176 total active
  • Meta: 1,555 initial FY2025 (+112% vs FY2023) · 4,740 continuing
  • Microsoft: 1,394 initial FY2025 · 4,863 continuing
  • Google: 1,050 initial FY2025 · 4,509 continuing
  • ~70% Master's holders across all four sponsors
  • Big Three ownership — Amazon: Vanguard 7.86% / BlackRock 6.83% / State Street 3.61%
  • Big Three ownership — Meta: 7.91% / 6.78% / 3.59% · Microsoft: 9.67% / 8.11% / 4.12%
  • H-1B approvals vs. layoffs: Amazon +20% H-1B / ~30,000 layoffs · Meta +112% H-1B / ~21,000 layoffs
  • Federal contracts: Amazon/AWS ~$798M FY2022–2025 · Microsoft ~$532M
  • State subsidies: Amazon $11.6B+ since ~2000 · Google $2.319B · Microsoft $1.602B
  • Lobbying 2025: Meta $26.29M · Amazon $18.87M · Google $16.54M · Microsoft $10.11M
  • All four donated $1M each to Trump 2025 inaugural committee
Outsourcing / Staffing Firms

Indian IT services firms — TCS, Cognizant, Infosys, Wipro, HCL — act as middlemen. They sponsor H-1B visas but deploy workers to third-party end-client companies. The outsourcer profits from the spread between what the client pays and what the worker receives. The worker's visa is tied to the outsourcer, not the end-client where they actually work.

  • TCS FY2025: 846 initial (down 42% from FY2024 peak of 1,452) · 5,293 continuing
  • Cognizant FY2025: 743 initial (down from 2,873 in FY2024) · 3,622 continuing
  • TCS worker education: 85.2% Bachelor's — contrast with direct sponsors (~70% Master's)
  • Cost savings delivered to end-clients: 30–40% below direct hire
  • HCL documented underpayment (EPI): $95 million wage theft across H-1B workers
  • Wage gap at outsourcing firms: up to 35% below comparable U.S. workers (NBER 2026)
  • DOJ Compunnel settlement April 2026: ads explicitly excluded U.S. citizens, favored H-1B holders
H-1B End-Client Companies

Major U.S. corporations that receive H-1B workers deployed by outsourcing firms like TCS, Cognizant, and Infosys. They do not sponsor the workers directly — they pay a service contract to the outsourcer and receive IT labor at 30–40% below the cost of a direct hire. The Big Three own large stakes in both the outsourcers and every major end-client.

  • TCS's largest documented end-client: Citigroup — ~3,000 new H-1B contractors placed 2020–2024
  • Other documented TCS end-clients: Capital One, Verizon, AT&T, Walmart, JPMorgan Chase, Goldman Sachs
  • Big Three ownership — JPMorgan: Vanguard 9.91% / BlackRock 7.89% / State Street 4.67%
  • Big Three ownership — Goldman Sachs: 9.84% / 7.88% / 6.63% · Verizon: 9.08% / 8.83% / 5.33%
  • Labor cost savings: 30–40% below direct hire cost — margin gains flow to share price
  • The Big Three own both the outsourcing firm and every major end-client receiving the workers
H-1B Workers

Approximately 730,000 H-1B workers are active in the U.S. as of early 2026. Predominantly Indian nationals, most holding Master's degrees, working in technology, finance, and professional services. The visa tether — one employer controls immigration status — creates a structural power imbalance regardless of skill level or compensation.

  • ~730,000 active in U.S. early 2026
  • India: 71–73% of approvals · China: 11–12%
  • 16% wage gap vs. comparable U.S. workers after controls (NBER w34793, Feb 2026)
  • At outsourcing firms: wage gap reaches up to 35%
  • Average offered wages ~$10,191 below OEWS mean for similar occupations
  • 60-day deportation clock starts day after last paid work
  • India EB-2 green card wait: priority date ~2013 = 12+ year backlog (Visa Bulletin Feb 2026)
  • Bench time: non-billable periods = reduced or no pay while still tied to sponsor
  • Illegal recruiter fees: hundreds to thousands upfront, prohibited but common
  • 4× more likely to live in overcrowded housing than U.S. natives
  • Annual remittances: $15,000–$25,000+ per worker
U.S. Tech Workers

American workers in technology and professional services who compete for roles in H-1B-heavy industries. The effect is not only direct displacement — LCA wage floors set below market rates suppress wages across entire departments even for workers who keep their jobs. The presence of visa-tethered colleagues also undermines collective organizing.

  • 85,000 Americans laid off; 34,000 H-1B workers hired — same companies, same years (Sen. Sanders, Jan 2025)
  • Disney 2015: 250 IT workers laid off and forced to train their H-1B replacements from HCL/Cognizant
  • Southern California Edison 2015: 500 workers displaced by H-1B outsourcing
  • Average offered H-1B wages ~$10,191 below OEWS mean — suppresses entire department wages
  • LCA wage Level I/II structurally anchors pay below market — affects U.S. workers in the same roles
  • Captive visa workforce reduces ability to organize — colleagues facing deportation cannot safely participate
American People

The 128 million Americans whose retirement savings — through 401(k)s, IRAs, and pension funds — are managed by the Big Three. These same households absorb the wage suppression and displacement effects of the H-1B program through their labor market participation, while simultaneously funding the asset managers who profit from it through their retirement accounts.

  • 128 million U.S. investors — 56.4% of all U.S. households
  • 73% hold accounts through employer-sponsored plans (401k, pension)
  • 20–25% median voting power transferred to Big Three at any S&P 500 meeting
  • Return loop: 401(k)/IRA contributions → Big Three AUM → proxy votes aligned with H-1B sponsors
  • BlackRock pension AUM alone: $3.9 trillion (Q1 2026 earnings)
  • ERISA §403(a)(2): proxy voting is manager's fiduciary duty — participant has no vote
