Section 02 — Institutional Asset Managers

What Is an Asset Manager?

What Is an Asset Manager?

BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street manage retirement accounts for more than half of all U.S. households — 128 million individual investors. When you signed up for your 401(k), buried in the terms and conditions nobody reads, you handed them something far more valuable than your money: your vote.

Every share of stock in your retirement account comes with a vote in how that company is run — who sits on the board, how executives get paid, what labor policies get approved. You gave those votes away without knowing it. Together, the Big Three control roughly three-quarters of the U.S. equity ETF market and cast votes at corporate elections in nearly every major American company — using your money, without asking you.

No government. No union. No activist group comes close to that concentration of power. And it runs entirely on the retirement savings of ordinary Americans who have no idea it's happening.

Where Their Money Comes From
How your retirement contributions — and taxpayer-backed public funds — became their power base
﹀

Most Americans never write a check to BlackRock, Vanguard, or State Street. The money flows automatically. When you — or your employer — contribute to a 401(k), 403(b), IRA, or pension plan, those dollars typically flow into low-cost index funds overwhelmingly managed by the Big Three.

But private retirement savings are only part of the story. An equally large and largely invisible channel runs through government-mandated systems.

How You Gave Away Your Vote Without Knowing It
What shareholder voting is — and why you no longer do it
﹀

Every share of stock comes with voting rights. At a company's annual shareholder meeting, owners vote on board directors, executive compensation packages, mergers, and major policy decisions that shape corporate behavior — including labor strategy and hiring practices.

In theory, if your retirement account holds those shares, you should have a say. In practice, you signed that right away the moment your 401(k), 403(b), IRA, or pension plan invested in an index fund.

Your money buys the shares. The law hands the vote to the manager. You are never consulted.

When your plan buys shares through a mutual fund or ETF, the legal right to vote those shares — called proxy voting — transfers to the fund manager. Under ERISA section 403(a)(2) and long-standing DOL guidance, proxy voting is a core fiduciary duty of the investment manager, not the individual participant. Standard plan documents make this delegation automatic. Almost no one reads them.

✓ Verified — DOL Federal Register, December 2020

The Department of Labor has repeatedly confirmed that managing shareholder rights — including proxy voting — is a fiduciary responsibility under ERISA. When authority is delegated to an investment manager, day-to-day voting power rests with the asset manager. The same index funds holding your retirement savings give BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street the legal authority to cast votes at corporate elections — on executive pay, board composition, and labor policy — without ever consulting you.

What They Do With That Voting Power
Block voting mechanics and how boards learn to respond
﹀

The Big Three are the largest shareholder in roughly 88% of S&P 500 companies. Collectively, they control 20–25% of the vote at any given shareholder meeting. No other single institution comes close.

They do not need to coordinate. Because they all hold index funds that mirror the same broad market, their economic incentives are structurally identical: whatever increases long-term portfolio-wide returns benefits all three. This produces natural voting alignment — no back-room agreement required.

The Scale of Concentrated Voting Power
The further up the pyramid, the more concentrated the power — all of it built on the savings of the people at the base.
✓ Verified — Morningstar 2025

In the 2025 proxy season, the Big Three backed management proposals 98.7% of the time on average. Boards and CEOs quickly learn which behaviors earn consistent support: lower labor costs, higher margins, and strategies that protect profit — including aggressive use of guest-worker programs when they deliver cost savings. The feedback loop is simple and powerful. Consistent voting rewards companies that deliver what the portfolio values.

Why They Always Vote the Same Way — Without Talking to Each Other
Cross-ownership aligns their incentives automatically. No conspiracy required.
﹀

Vanguard is BlackRock's largest shareholder, owning about 9% of the company — roughly $15 billion worth of shares as of early 2026. BlackRock owns about 6.5% of its own shares through its funds. State Street owns about 4% of BlackRock. BlackRock, in turn, holds about 8% of State Street.

This is not a secret plot. Here is why it happens:

Index funds must own a little bit of every big company in the same proportion as the overall stock market. BlackRock is itself a large publicly traded company — so Vanguard's index funds are required to own BlackRock stock. BlackRock's funds must own State Street stock. And so on. The structure creates the circle automatically.

The Cross-Ownership Circle
Index funds must own everything in proportion — so they end up owning each other. No coordination needed. The structure does it automatically.
Source: SEC 13F filings Q4 2025 / early 2026 — Fintel, WhaleWisdom, company disclosures

Because they all own pieces of the same giant portfolio, they all want exactly the same thing — higher profits across the whole market. That means they naturally vote the same way on wages, labor costs, and guest-worker programs. No secret meetings. No explicit agreement. The incentive structure does the work for them.

How They Influence Policy Without Lobbying For It
Their portfolio companies lobby the laws — the managers vote the profits
﹀

BlackRock spent $3.4 million on federal lobbying in 2025. Vanguard spent $2.58 million. Almost none of it targeted immigration or H-1B/H-2 visa bills directly. Their lobbying stays focused on securities rules and investment policy.

They don't need to lobby immigration law themselves. Their portfolio companies — Amazon, Google, Microsoft, the big outsourcing firms, and groups like the U.S. Chamber of Commerce — do that heavy lifting. Once those policies become law and low-cost visa labor becomes available, the asset managers simply use their massive aligned voting blocks to reward the companies that use visas most aggressively. Lower labor costs → higher margins → higher stock prices. The funds perform better. Management fees go up.
